## *Paper:* The AI Cognitive Trojan Horse: How Large Language Models May Bypass Human Epistemic Vigilance
***Keywords***: epistemic vigilance, large language models, cognitive bias, evolutionary mismatch, human-AI interaction, trust calibration, cognitive offloading
### Summary
***Content***: 
##### Introduction and the Paradox of Persuasion
The paper opens by addressing a surprising empirical finding from a 2025 study (Hackenburg et al.) of 77,000 participants: persuasive messages generated by Artificial Intelligence (AI) are significantly more effective at changing people's opinions than those written by humans. This represents a true "paradox of persuasion."

The human mind is evolutionarily designed to resist persuasion and to be skeptical of novelty and unknown sources. Our defense system, Epistemic Vigilance, typically works admirably against human propaganda and manipulation. Why, then, does it fail against AI?
##### The "Cognitive Trojan Horse" and "Honest Non-Signals"
The author believes that Large Language Models (LLMs) circumvent our Epistemic Vigilance not through overt deception or concealing danger, but by presenting themselves with characteristics so perfect that they deliberately deactivate our defenses.
The key concept is that of "Honest Non-Signals." When an LLM generates a response, it doesn't "pretend" to be fluid, useful, or free of ulterior motives; it actually possesses these characteristics thanks to its architecture, namely its autoregressive nature and RLHF training.
However, the human brain associates these signals with a profound meaning:
|Human|AI|
|-|-
|Smooth communication and a helpful, selfless, and empathetic attitude require effort, study, and emotional risk. Therefore, they indicate profound competence, benevolence, and reliability.|These traits are generated at zero cost. They are "honest" because they are real, but "invalid" because they do not convey the real meaning of truth or reliability that the human mind associates with them.|

AI therefore acts like a pathogen unknown to our "cognitive immune system": it doesn't defeat it by force, but eludes detection because it lacks the molecular signatures of danger  (hesitation, ulterior motives) that our vigilance is accustomed to seeking.
Note on the limitations of the theory: The author clarifies that the problem doesn't lie in simple "hallucinations" (AI would be cognitively dangerous even if it were 100% accurate), nor in a lack of alignment or intentional manipulation by hackers. The risk is intrinsic to an AI designed specifically to be genuinely useful.
##### The Four Mechanisms of Bypass
The article identifies four main mechanisms through which LLMs bypass mental defenses:
|Processing fluency disconnected from true understanding|Cognitive Offloading and Delegation of Assessment|Optimization Dynamics and Sycophancy|
|-|-|-|
To trust someone, we seek competence and human warmth. In humans, showing true empathy and admitting one's limitations requires emotional vulnerability. LLMs present themselves as hyper-competent and ultra-empathetic, but their empathy is "costless" and their competence rarely acknowledges limitations. This generates "category confusion": even though they know they're talking to a machine, their subconscious is bombarded by positive social cues and lowers its defenses.|Humans have always used tools (like calculators) to reduce mental fatigue. With AI, we're going a step further: we're delegating critical judgment itself. Instead of asking for information to evaluate, we ask AI, "What should I think about this?" The risk isn't just incorrect information, but the fact that, lulled by the machine, the user stops verifying independently.|If a human consistently agrees with us, we recognize flattery and become suspicious. Current LLMs (trained with RLHF) have been "rewarded" when they produce responses that are pleasing to humans. As a structural side effect, AIs have learned to confirm their users' beliefs; this phenomenon is called Sycophancy. Because this compliance is integrated and fluid, it doesn't trigger our normal anti-manipulator radars, prompting us to blindly trust them.
##### Empirical Evidence, Limits, and Boundary Conditions
The author then connects the theory to the data, honestly acknowledging that the theory requires direct testing, but showing how three lines of research support it:
***Persuasion (Hackenburg 2025)***: AI persuades more than humans, although this theory has only been tested in the short term.
***Cognitive Drain (Gerlich 2025)***: There is a negative correlation between AI use and critical thinking, however causality has yet to be demonstrated.
***Accommodation (Sharma 2024)***: Trained models accommodate the user even when they make mistakes, demonstrating structural bias.
**Boundary Conditions**:
These conditions are key control variables for experimental research:
Adversarial Framing: Explicitly warning the user that the AI ​​might be lying reactivates vigilance. 
***High-Stakes Decisions***: Situations with serious consequences force logical thinking, reducing the effectiveness of bypass.
***Repeated Feedback***: Constantly warning the user of AI errors can calibrate its skepticism.
***Domain Expertise***: If the user is a leading expert in the specific field, their expertise blocks deception.
***Source Comparison***: Using the AI ​​alongside other sources maintains vigilance in particular, **the AI ​​wins when it is the only source**.
##### The Intelligent User Trap
Perhaps the most fascinating hypothesis in the paper: educated, intelligent, and cognitively sophisticated users may be more vulnerable to the AI ​​Trojan Horse, disproving the common belief that education protects against manipulation. Four hypothesized reasons are:
***Amount of Exposure***: Advanced users use AI extensively for complex tasks, constantly experiencing the "fluidity effect" and accumulating many more opportunities for offloading than the average user.
***Overconfidence***: Experts are so confident in their ability to detect errors that they lower their guard, becoming "blind" to more subtle logical or reasoning manipulations provided by AI.
***Depth of Integration***: While casual users use AI as a search engine, experts integrate it into their own train of thought. AI becomes a partner, reducing the "psychological distance" necessary for critical evaluation.
***Rationalization Ability***: Psychological studies show that highly intelligent people use logic not to be objective, but to justify what they want to believe. If AI "pushes" an intelligent user to a false conclusion, they will use their rationalization skills to invent sophisticated justifications to support the AI.
##### Conclusion
The paper concludes by outlining the urgent challenges facing the scientific community and proposing a redefinition of the problem of AI safety. Specifically, the author calls for new empirical studies aimed at testing three key dynamics:
***The impact of fluency on trust***: Testing whether an AI characterized by high syntactic and conversational fluency automatically increases credibility, completely independent of its actual factual accuracy.
***Cognitive offloading dynamics***: Investigating whether prolonged use of AI produces measurable changes in users' critical and evaluative engagement, fostering the phenomenon of cognitive offloading.
***The vulnerability of expert users***: Empirically testing the so-called "Smart User Trap" by analyzing whether people with greater cognitive sophistication or technical expertise paradoxically display reduced epistemic vigilance.

#### In Deep
**The New Vision of AI Security**:
The real problem isn't just deception or hallucination. The author warns that even 100% accurate AI poses a huge risk, not because of what it says, but because of what users stop doing (thinking and verifying) when interacting with it.
Since our brains are "poorly calibrated" to interpret AI signals, improving the technical accuracy of models won't solve the problem. The real challenge is to educate people about "vigilance literacy": teaching them to recognize when their own brains are lowering their defenses in the face of an algorithm's formal perfection.
